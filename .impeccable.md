# enuff-is-enuff-unsubscribe — Design Context

## Users

People drowning in recurring email noise — newsletters they never read, ecommerce promos, abandoned SaaS drips, creator/course launches, conference promos, "we miss you" campaigns. Skewed tech-aware (they use Claude Code), but the pain is universal. Reading `getting-started.html` on a laptop while deciding whether to install. Mood: irritated, ready for relief, skeptical of yet-another-tool-that-promises-to-fix-this.

Job to be done: in 20 minutes, identify the senders that have been yelling at me for years and shut them up — without accidentally killing receipts or security alerts.

## Brand Personality

Three words: **blunt · relieving · no-bullshit**.

Inspired by `just-fucking-cancel` energy. The product is named "**Enuff Is Enuff**" — that's the tone. Painkiller, not wellness. Tool, not platform. Privacy-forward (local-first, no hosted inbox dragnet). Should feel like the guy who says "yeah, that's broken, here's how I fix it" — not the smiling SaaS landing page promising delight.

Emotional goal: the reader feels seen ("yes, my inbox does this to me"), then capable ("I can actually do something about it in one sitting"), then trusting ("this won't ask for my password or upload my mail to a server").

## Aesthetic Direction

**Editorial / Manifesto** — big typographic statement at the top, longform-essay rhythm, asymmetric, generous whitespace, deliberately unfashionable. Reads like a quietly authoritative magazine piece on inbox civility, not a marketing page.

- **Theme**: light mode. Warm cream paper background, ink-black type, single oxblood accent for emphasis (the "STOP" color, used sparingly).
- **Typography**: editorial serif display + a no-nonsense sans body. Civic / public-interest typography — closer to a USDS document or *The Browser* newsletter than a startup splash. Specifically: **Spectral** (display, free) + **Public Sans** (body, USDS, free). Both Google Fonts; neither in the AI-default rejection list.
- **Color**: OKLCH throughout. Warm-tinted neutrals (every gray has a hint of the paper-cream hue). Oxblood red as the only accent — used for the brand mark, key emphasis, and the "stop" semantic.
- **Layout**: asymmetric grid, generous gutters, content max-width capped at ~70ch for readability. Numbered steps as oversized numerals (not pill-icons). Pull-quotes from the brand voice ("Your inbox has 412 strangers in it. That's enough.").
- **Voice in copy**: short, declarative, unsentimental. No exclamation marks. No "Get started today!" energy.

## Anti-References

- ❌ Wellness / mindfulness app palettes (sage, beige, muted earth tones used to signal "calm")
- ❌ Tech startup landing pages (gradient hero, hero metric tile, big rounded-corner cards in a 3-up grid)
- ❌ "Modern minimalism" cliches (Inter, big logo, single sentence headline, illustration)
- ❌ Glassmorphism, gradient text, side-stripe borders, sparklines as decoration
- ❌ Dark mode with neon accents (the "AI dashboard" look)
- ❌ Emoji icons, pastel illustrations, smiling product personas

## Design Principles

1. **Editorial over marketing.** The page is a piece of writing, not a pitch. Reads top-to-bottom like a column.
2. **Bluntness as luxury.** The most refined thing on the page is its refusal to be cute or persuasive.
3. **One color does all the work.** Oxblood red shows up rarely; when it does, it means business.
4. **Type carries the design.** No illustrations, no icons-on-cards, no decorative chrome. The hierarchy is built entirely from typography and whitespace.
5. **Shippable, not impressive.** Production-grade: no JS dependencies, no web fonts loaded from third parties without `font-display: swap`, no layout shift, accessible contrast, mobile-fluid via clamp().
